## The Werewolves Family

### The Werewolves of Miller's Hollow — Base Game (2001)

The canonical Werewolves game. 8–18 players, requires a human moderator. Players are secretly assigned roles; the village votes out suspects by day while Werewolves eliminate Villagers by night. The Seer is the village's main information source. Nominated for Spiel des Jahres 2003.

**First-impression roles:** Werewolf, Villager, Seer, Witch, Hunter, Cupid, Little Girl, Sheriff (elected by vote)

---

### The Werewolves of Miller's Hollow — New Moon (2006 expansion)

Adds roles with more complex win conditions and night abilities. Notable for introducing roles that have their own solo win condition (e.g., Pied Piper), making the game a multi-faction game rather than a pure good-vs-evil.

**First-impression roles:** Angel, Bear Tamer, Fox, Pied Piper, Scapegoat, Wild Child

---

### The Werewolves of Miller's Hollow — The Village (2009 expansion)

Focuses on village structure and adds location-based mechanics. Introduces the idea that the village itself has identifiable buildings and a social hierarchy.

**First-impression roles:** Mayor, Gravedigger, Knight (Rusty Sword)

---

### The Werewolves of Miller's Hollow — Characters (2012 expansion)

A role-heavy expansion with a large cast. Many roles are defined by triggering on specific events (death of a neighbor, end of a vote, etc.).

**First-impression roles:** Stuttering Judge, Gypsy, Devoted Servant, Two Sisters, Three Brothers

---

### The Werewolves of Miller's Hollow — The Pact (2014 expansion)

Adds further complexity and is often considered the "complete" edition when combined with earlier sets.

**First-impression roles:** *(to be researched in Phase 2)*

---

### One Night Ultimate Werewolf (2014)

Solves the eliminaton problem: the entire game plays in a single night phase followed by one vote. No player is ever sitting out. Uses a smartphone app as narrator/moderator — directly relevant to this app's value proposition. Much faster (10–15 minutes per game), often played in repeated sessions.

**First-impression roles:** Werewolf, Seer, Robber, Troublemaker, Drunk, Insomniac, Doppelganger

---

### One Night Ultimate Werewolf — Daybreak (expansion)

Adds new roles, many of which favor the werewolf team or are neutral. Designed to mix with the base One Night set.

**First-impression roles:** Alpha Wolf, Mystic Wolf, Apprentice Seer, Aura Seer, Paranormal Investigator, Revealer

---

### Ultimate Werewolf (2008)

The longer, multi-night version closer to Miller's Hollow in structure but with many more roles and supports up to 75 players. Requires a dedicated moderator/host.

**First-impression roles:** Werewolf, Villager, Seer, Vampire (third faction), Vigilante, Bodyguard

## Other Popular Social Deduction Games

### Mafia (1986)

The original social deduction game, created as a psychology experiment in Moscow. No roles beyond Mafia and Innocent Citizens in the base form. Pure deduction through discussion. The template that all Werewolf variants descend from.

**First-impression roles:** Mafioso, Innocent, (sometimes) Detective, Doctor

---

### The Resistance (2009) / Avalon (2012)

No player elimination — everyone plays every round. Players vote on whether to send proposed teams on missions; Resistance agents sabotage missions from inside. Avalon adds specific character roles. Very tight design that works especially well with 5–10 players.

**First-impression roles (Avalon):** Merlin, Percival, Loyal Servant of Arthur, Assassin, Morgana, Oberon, Mordred

---

### Secret Hitler (2016)

Hidden role game set in 1930s Germany. Liberals and Fascists debate and pass policies; the Fascists know each other but Hitler does not know who the Fascists are. Simple rules, very tense, scales to 10 players. Free print-and-play version made it viral.

**First-impression roles:** Liberal, Fascist, Hitler

---

### Blood on the Clocktower (2022)

Best-in-class Werewolf-style game with the key innovation that dead players still participate (ghost vote). Very high production quality, large role catalogue organized into Scripts (curated set of roles per game). Considered by many the peak of the genre.

**First-impression roles:** Washerwoman, Empath, Fortune Teller, Demon, Poisoner, Spy, Ravenkeeper

---

### Coup (2012)

Shorter, faster, more confrontational. Each player holds two hidden character cards; players bluff about which characters they have and challenge each other's claims. Eliminated when you lose both cards. Works perfectly with 2–6 players.

**First-impression roles:** Duke, Assassin, Captain, Ambassador, Contessa

---

### Spyfall (2014)

One player is the Spy with no idea of the secret location; all other players know it. Players ask each other questions; the Spy tries to deduce the location while the others try to identify the Spy. No elimination, fast and replayable.

**First-impression roles:** Spy, (all other players are location-specific characters, e.g., "doctor at the hospital")

---

### Deception: Murder in Hong Kong (2014)

One player is the Murderer with a hidden weapon and evidence clue; one is the Forensic Scientist who knows the murder cards but can only communicate through pictogram tiles. Investigators deduce the crime from indirect clues.

**First-impression roles:** Murderer, Forensic Scientist, Investigator, Witness, Accomplice

---

### Two Rooms and a Boom (2013)

Scales to very large groups (10–30+). Players split into two physical rooms with no communication between rooms. Each round, teams negotiate to swap hostages. One room has the President; the other has the Bomber. If they end up in the same room at the end, the Bomber's team wins.

**First-impression roles:** President, Bomber, Engineer (defuses), Hostage Taker, Ambassador
